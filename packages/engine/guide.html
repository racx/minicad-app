MINICAD — Beginner's Guide

Everything you need to draw a floor plan, no CAD experience required.

Opening the app

Double-click index.html and it opens in Safari. (Chrome refuses to load it directly from a file — if you prefer Chrome, start the little server first: python3 serve.py in the MiniCAD folder, then open localhost:8000.)

The one big idea

MiniCAD works like professional CAD software: you type short commands instead of hunting through menus. The bar at the bottom of the screen is the command line — it's always listening. Type L and press Enter, and you're drawing lines. That's it, that's the whole trick.

The three sacred keys:
Enter (or Space, or right-click) — means "yes / done / next".
Esc — means "never mind, cancel". When in doubt, press Esc and start over.
Enter on an empty command line — repeats the last command. Very handy when drawing lots of the same thing.

The yellow text next to the command line always tells you what the program expects next ("Specify first point:", "Specify radius:"…). Read the yellow prompt — it's the app talking to you.

Your first drawing: a room

Let's draw a 4 m × 3 m room with a doorway, a sofa and a label. We'll pretend one unit = 1 cm, so the room is 400 × 300. (Units are whatever you decide they are — the program doesn't care.)

  1. Make sure ORTHO and OSNAP are lit up (green) in the bottom row. Click them to toggle. ORTHO keeps your lines perfectly horizontal/vertical.
  2. Type L Enter. Click anywhere on the canvas to place the first corner.
  3. Move the mouse to the right (don't click) and type 400 Enter. A 400-long wall appears in the direction of your mouse. This is called direct distance entry — point the mouse, type the length.
  4. Mouse up, type 300 Enter. Mouse left, type 400 Enter.
  5. For the last wall, just hover over the very first corner — a little yellow square (▪) appears, meaning "I'll snap exactly onto that point" — and click. Press Enter to finish the LINE command.
  6. Type Z Enter then E Enter to center the room on screen.
  7. Cut a doorway in the bottom wall: draw two short vertical lines crossing it, about 90 apart (L, click above the wall, click below it, Enter — twice). Then type TR Enter (Trim), press Enter again (meaning "everything is a knife"), and click the piece of bottom wall between the two little lines. It disappears. Enter to finish.
  8. Erase the two helper lines: click one, click the other (they highlight blue), type E Enter.
  9. Furniture: switch the LAYER dropdown (top-right) to furniture. Type REC Enter, click two opposite corners to make a sofa. Type C Enter, click a center point, type 30 Enter — a side table.
  10. Label it: switch layer to annot. Type T Enter, click where the text should start, type 20 Enter for the height, then type Living room Enter.
  11. Click Save in the toolbar — it downloads drawing.json. Next time, click Open and pick that file to continue where you left off.

Congratulations — you've used coordinates, snapping, trimming and layers. That's 90% of real drafting.

Drawing commands

TypeWhat it does
LLine. Click point after point; each click adds a wall segment. Enter to stop.
PLPolyline. Like Line, but the segments stay together as one object. Type C to close the shape.
RECRectangle. Click two opposite corners.
CCircle. Click the center, then click the edge — or type the radius as a number.
AArc. A curve through three clicks: where it starts, somewhere along it, where it ends. For door swings and curved walls.
TText. Click a point, type a height (bigger number = bigger letters), type the words. Made a typo? Double-click any text later to rewrite it.
DIMDimension. The measuring label: click two points (say, both ends of a wall), then click where the measurement line should sit. The number updates by itself if you later stretch the wall. Numbers too small or too big? Select the dimensions, type DIMTXT and give a height (or A to go back to automatic).

Editing commands

These all work the same way: select things first, then type the command — or type the command first and it will ask you to select. Click objects to select them, or drag a box around several (see "Selecting" below).

TypeWhat it does
MMove. Pick a grab point, then where it should go. (Shortcut: once something is selected, you can also just drag it with the mouse — use M when you need precision, dragging when you're arranging furniture by eye.)
COCopy. Same as Move but the original stays. Keeps placing copies until you press Enter.
RORotate. Pick a pivot point, then type an angle like 90 or click.
SCScale. Pick a base point, type a factor: 2 = double, 0.5 = half.
OOffset. Makes a parallel copy at a set distance — perfect for wall thickness. Type the distance, click the object, click which side. Works on whole rectangles too: offset a room inward by 12 and you have both faces of the wall in one move.
TRTrim. Cuts pieces off where things cross. Select the "knives" and press Enter (or just press Enter right away = everything is a knife), then click the parts you want gone.
EXExtend. The opposite of Trim — grows a line until it reaches another one. Press Enter first (= anything can be the wall it grows toward), then click near the end of the line you want to stretch.
FFillet. Joins two lines into a neat corner — even if they don't touch yet. It first asks for a radius: press Enter for a sharp corner (radius 0), or type something like 10 for a rounded one. Then click the two lines, on the sides you want to keep. It remembers the radius for next time.
MIMirror. Flip a copy across a line — perfect for symmetric rooms. Select things, click two points for the "mirror", then answer whether to keep the original (just press Enter = keep).
SStretch. Make a room longer without redrawing it: drag a right-to-left box over one end, Enter, then a from-point and a to-point. Corners inside the box move; everything else stays anchored.
EErase. Deletes the selection. The Delete key works too.
UUndo. Your best friend. REDO brings it back. Ctrl-Z works too.
DIDistance. Click two points, it tells you how far apart they are. Measuring tape.

Drawing precisely (the magic part)

Snapping (OSNAP)

With OSNAP on, the cursor magnetically grabs exact points on things you've already drawn, and shows a yellow marker telling you what it grabbed:

▪ endpoint △ midpoint ○ center of a circle ◇ quadrant (top/bottom/left/right of a circle) ✕ where two things cross ⊥ perpendicular (square angle onto a line) ⊼ tangent (kisses a circle)

This is how you make walls that actually meet. Trust the yellow marker — if you see it, your click lands exactly there.

The app also volunteers suggestions while you draw: when your line is about to cross another one, the ✕ marker jumps to the exact crossing point — click and they meet perfectly. And when your cursor lines up with an existing corner, a dashed teal guide appears from that corner; click while the guide shows and you're exactly aligned (two guides at once = you're at the projected corner).

Choosing which snaps run (type OSNAP)

If snapping keeps grabbing points you don't want — say, midpoints while you're chasing endpoints — type OSNAP (or OS) and a small picker opens with a checkbox for every marker. Untick what's in your way; your choices are remembered on this browser. Two extras live there too:

⧖ NearestGrab any point along an edge, not just the special ones. Off out of the box (it makes every hover sticky) — tick it when you need it.
Alignment trackingThe dashed teal guides described above. Untick if they distract you.

When two snaps are in reach at once, the more specific one wins (endpoint beats midpoint beats center) — zoom in a little if the app grabs the wrong one. F3 still switches all snapping on/off in one hit.

Hatching & areas (type H)

Type H, pick a material from the little catalog — concrete, brick, green area, window glass, wood, water — then click inside a closed shape (a room, a slab, a garden). The shape fills with that material's pattern and the command history reports its area and perimeter in your drawing units. Hatch the same shape again to swap materials.

Type AREA (or AA) and click any hatch or closed shape to measure it again — handy for room schedules. Hatches follow their outline when you move or stretch it, and disappear with it when you erase it. The outline must be closed: if your walls are separate lines, JOIN them into a loop first.

Typing coordinates

Whenever the app asks for a point, you can type instead of clicking:

400Direct distance: go 400 units in the direction your mouse is pointing (the everyday workhorse)
100,50The exact spot x=100, y=50
@50,050 to the right of the last point
@100<45100 units away at a 45° angle from the last point

The toggle row (bottom of the screen)

ORTHOLocks drawing to horizontal/vertical. On for walls, off for angles. Key: F8
OSNAPThe magnetic snapping described above. Leave it on. Key: F3
GRIDShows the background grid. Key: F7
SNAPMakes the cursor jump in grid steps. Key: F9
Mac keyboard note: the F-keys may be busy doing volume/brightness. Either hold fn while pressing them, or simply click the ORTHO/OSNAP buttons at the bottom — same thing.

Layers (colored pens)

Think of layers as different colored pens: walls, furniture, annot (notes/labels). The dropdown at the top-right picks which pen you're drawing with; the color swatch changes its color; + adds a new one. Keeping walls and furniture on separate layers keeps a plan readable.

The 👁 button hides the chosen layer (great for seeing the bare walls without furniture), and 🔒 locks it — still visible and snappable, but click-proof, so you can't nudge the walls by accident while arranging chairs. Drew something on the wrong layer? Select it and type CH to move it.

Saving your work

SaveDownloads drawing.json — this is your file, reopen it with the Open button.
AutosaveThe app quietly saves your work in the browser every few seconds. Close the tab, reopen — your drawing comes back on its own. Type NEW for a fresh sheet. (Still press Save for a real file you can keep or share.)
OpenLoads a previously saved drawing.json
Export DXFMakes a .dxf file that opens in AutoCAD, LibreCAD, or any professional CAD program — for when you need to send your plan to an architect or a kitchen company.

Printing to scale

First tell the app what your numbers mean: type UNITS and answer cm (or mm, or m). Then press ⌘P (or type PLOT):

  1. Pick the paper (A4/A3/Letter) and orientation.
  2. Pick a scale — 1:50 is the classic for floor plans (1 cm on paper = 50 cm real). Or choose fit window and the app shows you what scale it managed.
  3. Pick window lets you click two corners around the part you want on paper.
  4. Press Print… — in the browser dialog keep scale at 100%, or choose Save as PDF for a file that stays true to scale.
Trust but verify: the Test page button prints a ruler — a 100 mm bar and a 50 mm bar. Print it once at 100% and measure with a real ruler. If the bars measure true, every plan you print will too.

The print is clean black-on-white (layer colors optional), with a footer showing the scale, paper and date — so whoever receives it knows exactly what they're holding.

When things go wrong

Nothing respondsA command is probably waiting for input — read the yellow prompt, or press Esc.
Lines fly off diagonallyORTHO is off. Click it (or F8).
Clicks won't land where I wantOSNAP is grabbing a nearby point. Toggle it off (F3), click, toggle it back on.
I can't see my drawingType Z Enter, E Enter — zoom extents brings it all back on screen.
I ruined everythingU Enter, as many times as needed. Breathe.
Blank page in ChromeOpen index.html with Safari instead (or run python3 serve.py).
A feature seems missingThe browser may be showing an old cached version — press ⌘⇧R (hard reload).
